Module 5: Real-World AI Workflows

Meeting Prep and Follow-Ups

Meetings consume an enormous chunk of every workweek. Depending on the study you look at, the average professional spends somewhere between 15 and 25 hours per week in meetings. And here is the uncomfortable truth: most of that time is poorly spent. People show up unprepared, the conversation wanders, nobody writes down what was decided, and two weeks later everyone has a different memory of what happened.

AI cannot attend the meeting for you (not yet, anyway). But it can make every minute of meeting time dramatically more productive by helping you before, during, and after the conversation. In this lesson, you will build a complete meeting workflow that turns AI into your personal meeting assistant, from the first calendar invite to the final follow-up email.

Before the Meeting: Research and Briefing

Walking into a meeting unprepared is like showing up to an exam without studying. You might get through it, but you will not perform at your best. The good news is that AI can do hours of prep work for you in minutes.

Researching Attendees and Companies

When you are meeting with someone new, whether a potential client, a partner, or a new colleague, a little background research goes a long way. AI can synthesize publicly available information into a concise briefing.

Prompt: "I have a meeting tomorrow with Sarah Chen, VP of Marketing at GreenLeaf Technologies. Research what you can about her professional background and the company. Give me a one-page briefing that includes: her likely priorities based on her role, recent company news or developments, and three conversation starters that show I have done my homework."

For internal meetings, the research looks different but is equally valuable. You might ask AI to summarize the last quarter's project updates, pull together key metrics, or review the notes from your last conversation with that team.

Anticipating Questions and Preparing Responses

One of the most powerful uses of AI in meeting prep is thinking through what the other side might ask. This is especially useful before presentations, pitch meetings, or any conversation where you need to defend a position.

Prompt: "I am presenting a proposal to switch our company's project management tool from Asana to Monday.com. The audience is our operations team, who have used Asana for three years. What are the top 10 objections or tough questions they are likely to raise, and give me a concise, persuasive response to each one."

Pro Tip: When preparing for high-stakes meetings, ask AI to role-play as the other party. You can literally rehearse the conversation. Try: "Act as a skeptical CFO who is being pitched on a $50,000 software investment. Push back on everything I say so I can practice my responses."

Creating an Agenda with AI

A meeting without an agenda is a conversation without a destination. You might end up somewhere interesting, but more likely you will wander in circles. The problem is that writing a good agenda takes thought, and most people either skip it entirely or dash off something vague five minutes before the meeting starts.

AI excels at turning vague intentions into structured plans. Here is the difference:

Without AI: Your calendar says "Catch up with client" and that is all anyone knows going in.

With AI, try this prompt: "I have a 30-minute meeting with my client, Riverside Marketing Agency. We last spoke three weeks ago. At that time, we had just launched their new ad campaign and were waiting for initial performance data. I also remember they mentioned concerns about their Q2 budget. Turn this into a structured meeting agenda with time allocations, specific discussion points, and a clear desired outcome for each section."

A well-crafted AI response might produce something like:

  • Welcome and quick check-in (3 min) — Build rapport, ask about any pressing concerns
  • Campaign performance review (10 min) — Walk through initial metrics, compare to benchmarks, discuss early wins and areas to adjust
  • Q2 budget discussion (10 min) — Address their budget concerns, present options for optimization, agree on spending parameters
  • Next steps and action items (5 min) — Assign owners, set deadlines, confirm next meeting date
  • Buffer (2 min) — Wrap-up and any last questions

Notice how much more useful that is than "catch up with client." Everyone who receives that agenda walks in knowing exactly what will be discussed and what decisions need to be made.

Adapting Agendas to Meeting Types

Different meetings call for different structures. A brainstorming session needs open-ended exploration time. A status update needs quick reporting and exception-based discussion. A decision meeting needs clear options and a voting or consensus mechanism. Tell AI what type of meeting you are running and it will adjust the structure accordingly.

Prompt: "Create an agenda for a 45-minute team brainstorming session about improving our customer onboarding process. We have six attendees. Structure it so that quieter team members have a chance to contribute, and make sure we leave with at least three concrete ideas to test."

During the Meeting: Real-Time Notes

Taking notes during a meeting creates a frustrating trade-off: the more carefully you write things down, the less present you are in the actual conversation. AI-powered transcription tools are changing this equation entirely.

AI Transcription Tools

Tools like Otter.ai, Fireflies.ai, Microsoft Copilot, and Zoom's built-in AI assistant can join your meetings (virtual or in-person) and create real-time transcripts. Some of them even identify speakers, flag action items, and generate summaries on the fly.

If you are not using a dedicated transcription tool, you can still leverage AI during meetings by jotting quick shorthand notes and then expanding them afterward. The key is capturing enough raw material for AI to work with later.

Tips for Capturing Key Points

Whether you are using AI transcription or taking manual notes, focus on capturing these four things:

  1. Decisions made — What was agreed upon? This is the most important category and the one most often lost.
  2. Action items — Who committed to doing what, and by when?
  3. Key discussion points — What were the main arguments or considerations? You do not need a word-for-word transcript, just the substance.
  4. Open questions — What was raised but not resolved? These need to go on the agenda for the next meeting.
Pro Tip: If you are taking notes manually during a meeting, use a simple shorthand: mark decisions with [D], action items with [A], and open questions with [Q]. This makes it much faster for AI to parse your notes afterward.

After the Meeting: The Follow-Up Email

The follow-up email is where most meeting value is either captured or lost. A good follow-up email sent within a few hours cements what was discussed, holds people accountable to their commitments, and prevents the "I thought we agreed on something different" conversations that plague teams.

The challenge is that writing a thorough follow-up takes time, and by the end of a day packed with meetings, your energy for writing is depleted. This is where AI becomes invaluable.

Turning Messy Notes into Professional Follow-Ups

Here is a real-world example. Say your meeting notes look like this:

"talked about Q3 launch timeline - sarah says design wont be ready til aug 15 - mike pushing for aug 1 - compromised on aug 8 but need to check with dev team - budget approved for extra contractor - jen handling that - also discussed logo options, going with version B - need to finalize copy by next friday"

Those notes are messy, informal, and incomplete. But they contain all the raw material AI needs.

Prompt: "Here are my rough notes from today's Q3 product launch planning meeting with Sarah (Design Lead), Mike (Product Manager), and Jen (Operations). Turn these into a professional follow-up email with a friendly but businesslike tone. Organize it with clear sections for decisions made, action items with owners and deadlines, and any items that still need resolution. Here are my notes: [paste notes]"

AI will transform those scattered notes into a polished email with clear formatting, proper attribution of action items, and a professional tone. All you need to do is review it for accuracy and hit send.

The Speed Advantage

Sending a follow-up email within an hour of the meeting ending has an outsized impact on how professional and organized you appear. With AI handling the writing, you can realistically send follow-ups for every meeting on the same day. That consistency builds trust and makes you the person others rely on to keep things moving.

The Meeting Summary Template

One of the most useful things you can build with AI is a reusable prompt template for processing meeting notes. Instead of crafting a new prompt every time, you create one master template and simply drop in your notes.

Here is a template you can adapt to your needs:

Prompt Template: "You are a professional meeting summarizer. I will provide raw meeting notes. Extract and organize the following: (1) Meeting Overview - date, attendees, and purpose in one sentence. (2) Key Decisions - bullet list of everything that was decided, with who made or approved the decision. (3) Action Items - table format with columns for Task, Owner, Deadline, and Priority (High/Medium/Low). (4) Open Questions - anything raised but not resolved, with who needs to follow up. (5) Next Meeting - date and preliminary agenda items if discussed. If any deadline or owner is unclear from the notes, flag it explicitly so I can fill it in. Here are the notes: [paste notes here]"

This template works for virtually any type of meeting. Save it somewhere accessible (a note-taking app, a text expander, a pinned message in your tools) so you can use it instantly after every meeting.

Pro Tip: Create variations of this template for different meeting types. Your one-on-one summary template might include a "feedback given/received" section. Your sales call template might include "objections raised" and "next steps in the pipeline." Customization makes the output immediately actionable.

Recurring Meetings: Building Institutional Memory

Here is where the meeting workflow gets really powerful. Most teams have recurring meetings: weekly standups, monthly reviews, quarterly planning sessions. The problem with these meetings is that each one starts from scratch. Nobody remembers exactly what was said last time, action items fall through the cracks, and the same issues get discussed over and over.

AI can solve this by building what you might call institutional memory for your meetings.

The Continuity Method

The concept is simple: feed the summary from your previous meeting as context when preparing for the next one.

Prompt: "Here is the summary from our last weekly team meeting on March 15: [paste previous summary]. Our next meeting is this Friday, March 22. Based on the action items and open questions from last time, generate an agenda for this week's meeting. Flag any action items that were due before this meeting so we can check on their status. Also suggest any new topics that logically follow from last week's discussion."

This creates a chain of continuity. Each meeting builds on the last. Action items do not get lost because the AI-generated agenda explicitly calls them out. Open questions from three meetings ago do not vanish because they are carried forward until resolved.

Building a Meeting Knowledge Base

Over time, your collection of meeting summaries becomes a searchable knowledge base. Need to remember when a particular decision was made? Search your summaries. Want to track how a project evolved over six months of meetings? The summaries tell the story. Onboarding a new team member who needs to get up to speed? Hand them the last few months of summaries.

Prompt: "Here are summaries from our last eight weekly product meetings [paste summaries]. Create a high-level executive summary of the major themes, decisions, and progress over this two-month period. Highlight any recurring issues that keep coming up but have not been resolved."

The Complete Meeting Workflow

Let us put everything together into a repeatable, end-to-end system. Here is the workflow you can follow for any important meeting:

Step 1: Prepare (15-20 minutes before)

  • Ask AI to research attendees and their companies if this is an external meeting
  • Feed AI the context from previous meetings (if recurring) and ask for an updated agenda
  • Ask AI to anticipate likely questions or objections based on the agenda
  • Review and customize the AI-generated agenda, then share it with attendees

Step 2: Capture (during the meeting)

  • Use an AI transcription tool if available, or take shorthand notes using the [D], [A], [Q] system
  • Focus on being present in the conversation rather than writing everything down
  • At the end of the meeting, verbally confirm decisions and action items with the group

Step 3: Process (within 1 hour after)

  • Feed your notes or transcript to AI using your meeting summary template
  • Review the AI output for accuracy, fill in any flagged gaps
  • Generate a professional follow-up email from the summary
  • Send the follow-up to all attendees

Step 4: Archive and Prepare for Next Time

  • Save the meeting summary in your filing system (document, folder, project management tool)
  • Add any action items to your task management system with deadlines
  • If it is a recurring meeting, file the summary where you can easily retrieve it for next time

This entire workflow takes about 20 minutes of your time (the prep and the post-processing), but it transforms a potentially wasteful meeting into a productive, well-documented business interaction.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

As you start using AI for meeting workflows, watch out for these pitfalls:

Sending AI-generated follow-ups without reviewing them. AI can misinterpret your shorthand or attribute an action item to the wrong person. Always read the output before sending. A factual error in a follow-up email is worse than no follow-up at all.

Over-relying on transcription tools. Technology fails. Batteries die, internet connections drop, and recording permissions get declined. Always have a manual backup method, even if it is just jotting down the three most important takeaways.

Generating agendas but not sharing them in advance. The agenda only works if attendees see it before the meeting. Send it at least a few hours ahead so people can prepare. An agenda shared at the start of the meeting is better than nothing, but loses most of its prep value.

Ignoring the institutional memory loop. The biggest long-term value comes from connecting meetings to each other. If you only use AI for one-off summaries, you are missing the compounding benefit of continuity.

Pro Tip: Start small. Pick one recurring meeting and commit to using this full workflow for the next four weeks. Once it becomes second nature for that one meeting, expand to others. Trying to overhaul every meeting at once is a recipe for giving up.
Exercise: Put this workflow into practice with your next real meeting. Before the meeting, use AI to create a structured agenda with talking points and at least three anticipated questions from the other attendees. After the meeting, paste your notes into AI using the meeting summary template from this lesson, then generate a follow-up email. Compare how this meeting felt compared to one where you did no AI-assisted preparation. Notice the difference in how organized and confident you felt, and how much faster the follow-up went out.
