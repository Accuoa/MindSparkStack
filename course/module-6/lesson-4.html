Module 6: AI for Technical Tasks (No Experience Required)

Idea to Prototype in an Afternoon

You have had an idea rattling around in your head for weeks. Maybe it is an app that solves a problem you keep running into. Maybe it is a tool that would make your work easier. Maybe it is a small business concept you have been sketching on napkins. Whatever it is, there has always been this massive gap between "I have an idea" and "I have something I can show people."

That gap used to be measured in weeks or months. You would need to learn to code, or hire a developer, or spend countless hours wrestling with unfamiliar software. Most ideas died in that gap — not because they were bad ideas, but because the cost of finding out whether they were good was just too high.

AI has compressed that gap to hours. Not days. Not weeks. Hours. And you do not need to know how to code, design, or build anything technical. You just need to be able to describe what you want clearly enough for AI to help you build it. That is what this lesson is about: taking an idea from your head and turning it into something real that you can show to someone else before the end of the day.

What Is a Prototype (And What It Is Not)

Before we get into the how, let us make sure we agree on what we are building. A prototype is the minimum viable version of your idea — just enough to test whether the concept works and whether people respond to it. Think of it like a rough sketch compared to a finished painting.

A prototype is:

  • A working demonstration of your core idea
  • Something people can actually interact with or look at
  • Good enough to get real feedback from real people
  • Built in hours, not weeks
  • A learning tool that helps you figure out what works and what does not

A prototype is not:

  • A finished product ready for thousands of users
  • Something that needs to handle every edge case
  • Perfectly designed or polished
  • Built to last forever without changes
  • Worth spending more than a few hours on before getting feedback

The goal of a prototype is learning, not perfection. You are trying to answer one question: Does this idea work? Everything else — the polish, the scalability, the edge cases — comes later, and only if the answer to that question is yes.

Pro Tip: If your prototype looks too polished, you probably spent too long on it. The ugliest prototype that gets shown to five people is worth more than the beautiful one that sits on your laptop forever.

The Prototyping Mindset

The biggest obstacle to building a prototype is not technical skill. It is mindset. Most people stall because they are trying to build the finished version on their first attempt. That is like trying to write the final draft of a novel without making any notes first.

Here are the three principles that will keep you moving:

Speed Over Perfection

Your only goal right now is to get something working. It does not need to be elegant. It does not need to handle every scenario. If the button looks a little off, that is fine. If the layout is not quite right, that is fine. You are building a proof of concept, not shipping a product. Every minute you spend polishing is a minute you are not spending learning whether the idea is worth polishing in the first place.

Learning Over Polish

Every prototype teaches you something. Maybe you discover that the feature you thought was essential is actually confusing. Maybe you realize the real value is in a part of the idea you almost left out. These lessons only come from building something and putting it in front of people. You cannot think your way to these insights — you have to build your way there.

Showing Over Telling

There is a world of difference between saying "I have this idea for an app that..." and opening your laptop to say "Here, try this." When people can click buttons, scroll through screens, or interact with a working tool, they give you completely different feedback than when you just describe your idea. They notice things you never would have thought of. They ask questions that reshape your thinking. A prototype makes your idea real enough for other people to engage with.

Pro Tip: Set a hard time limit before you start. Tell yourself "I am spending exactly two hours on this, and then I am showing it to someone." The constraint forces you to focus on what matters and skip what does not.

Example: Building a Simple Web App

Let us walk through a real example. Say you have an idea for a recipe organizer — a simple web app where you can paste in a recipe URL or type in ingredients, and it organizes everything into a clean, searchable collection. Here is how you would build a working prototype in an afternoon using AI.

Step 1: Describe Your Idea Clearly

Start by telling AI exactly what you want. Be specific about what the user should be able to do, not about the technical details.

Prompt: "I want to build a simple web app for organizing recipes. The user should be able to type in a recipe name, a list of ingredients, and the instructions. Each recipe gets saved as a card that shows the name and a preview of the ingredients. There should be a search bar at the top that filters recipes by name or ingredient. Everything should be stored locally in the browser so there is no need for a database. Make it look clean and modern with a warm color scheme."

Notice what this prompt does well: it describes the user experience, not the technology. You do not need to say "use React" or "store data in localStorage." AI will figure out the technical approach. You just need to paint a clear picture of what someone would see and do.

Step 2: Get the First Version

AI will generate a complete HTML file (or a set of files) that you can open in your browser immediately. Save the code to a file, open it, and see what you get. It will not be perfect on the first try — and that is completely expected.

Maybe the layout looks different than you imagined. Maybe the search feature does not quite work the way you wanted. Maybe the colors are off. That is all fine. You now have something real to react to, which is infinitely better than having nothing.

Step 3: Iterate Through Conversation

Now comes the fun part. You refine the prototype by having a conversation with AI about what you want to change. This is where it starts to feel like magic.

Prompt: "This is great, but I want a few changes. First, make the recipe cards display in a grid layout, three across on desktop. Second, add a tag system so I can label recipes as breakfast, lunch, dinner, or snack, and then filter by those tags. Third, change the color of the header to a darker warm tone."

Each round of feedback gets you closer to your vision. You do not need to understand any of the code — you just describe what you see and what you want to be different, exactly like you would if you were giving feedback to a designer.

Step 4: Add One Surprise Feature

Once the basics work, try asking for something you were not sure was possible:

Prompt: "Can you add a feature where I paste a URL from any recipe website and it automatically extracts the recipe name, ingredients, and instructions? I want it to fill in the form automatically so I just have to click save."

Sometimes AI will deliver exactly what you ask for. Sometimes it will explain limitations and offer alternatives. Either way, you learn something about what your idea can become — and you learn it in minutes, not after weeks of development.

Pro Tip: Save each version of your prototype as you go. Name them something simple like "recipe-app-v1.html", "recipe-app-v2.html" and so on. That way if a change breaks something, you can always go back to the last working version.

Example: Creating a Tool with Spreadsheets and AI

Not every prototype needs to be a web app. If you are more comfortable with spreadsheets, you can build surprisingly powerful tools using Google Sheets and AI-generated formulas and scripts. This approach works especially well for business tools, calculators, dashboards, and data organizers.

The Scenario

Let us say you run a small freelance business and you want a tool that tracks your projects, calculates your effective hourly rate for each one, flags projects where you are earning below your target rate, and gives you a monthly summary dashboard. Here is how you would prototype it.

Step 1: Describe the Spreadsheet Structure

Prompt: "I need a Google Sheets setup for tracking freelance projects. I want one sheet called 'Projects' with columns for: project name, client name, total fee, estimated hours, actual hours, start date, end date, and status (active, completed, invoiced, paid). I want another sheet called 'Dashboard' that automatically shows: total revenue this month, average hourly rate across all projects, number of active projects, and a list of any projects where my effective hourly rate is below $75. Can you give me the formulas I need and explain where each one goes?"

AI will give you the exact formulas, tell you which cells to put them in, and explain what each one does. You copy them into your spreadsheet, and your dashboard comes to life immediately.

Step 2: Add Smart Features with Apps Script

Want to go further? Ask AI to write a Google Apps Script that automates parts of your workflow:

Prompt: "Can you write a Google Apps Script that does two things? First, when I change a project status to 'completed,' it automatically fills in today's date as the end date. Second, every Monday morning it sends me an email summary of my active projects and my earnings for the previous week."

You do not need to understand the script to use it. AI will give you the code, tell you exactly where to paste it in Google Sheets (Extensions > Apps Script), and walk you through setting up the trigger for the weekly email. In 15 minutes, you have automated workflow features that would have taken a developer hours to build.

Step 3: Make It Visual

Prompt: "I want to add a chart to my Dashboard sheet that shows my monthly revenue over the past 6 months as a bar chart. I also want conditional formatting on the Projects sheet so that rows turn light red when the effective hourly rate is below $75 and light green when it is above $100."

AI will walk you through creating the chart and give you the exact conditional formatting rules to apply. Now your spreadsheet does not just track data — it tells a story at a glance.

Pro Tip: Spreadsheet prototypes are especially powerful for business ideas because they let you play with real numbers immediately. You can test pricing models, forecast revenue, and model different scenarios before you commit to building anything more complex.

The Iteration Loop

Whether you are building a web app or a spreadsheet tool, the real power of AI prototyping comes from the iteration loop. This is the back-and-forth conversation where you refine your prototype in real time. Here is how to do it effectively.

Describe What You See, Not What Is Wrong with the Code

You do not need to diagnose technical problems. Just describe what is happening versus what you expected:

Prompt: "When I click the save button, nothing happens. I expected the recipe to appear in the list below the form."

Prompt: "The search bar works but it is case-sensitive. I want it to find recipes whether I type 'Pasta' or 'pasta'."

Prompt: "The dashboard numbers update when I add a new project, but the chart does not refresh until I close and reopen the sheet."

Request Changes in Plain Language

Think of AI as a collaborator who happens to know how to code. Give feedback the same way you would to a person:

Prompt: "Make the button bigger and change it to a blue color. Also move it to the right side of the form instead of below it."

Prompt: "Add a dark mode toggle in the top right corner. When dark mode is on, the background should be dark gray and the text should be white."

Prompt: "The font feels too small on mobile. Can you make the whole thing more readable on a phone screen?"

Build in Layers

Start with the core functionality and add features one at a time. This is important because if something breaks, you know exactly which change caused it. Here is a typical layering approach:

  1. Layer 1: Get the basic structure and core feature working
  2. Layer 2: Improve the visual design and layout
  3. Layer 3: Add secondary features (search, filters, sorting)
  4. Layer 4: Handle edge cases (what if someone enters nothing? what about very long text?)
  5. Layer 5: Polish the user experience (animations, loading states, helpful messages)

Most prototypes only need layers 1 through 3. Do not feel pressured to go further unless the early feedback tells you the idea is worth investing more time in.

Pro Tip: When AI makes a change that breaks something, do not panic. Just say: "That last change broke the search feature. Can you undo that specific change and try a different approach?" AI can usually isolate and fix the issue without starting over.

When to Stop Prototyping and Start Building

Prototyping is addictive. Once you see how fast AI can turn your ideas into reality, it is tempting to keep adding features forever. But there is a point where you need to make a decision: is this idea worth building for real?

Signs Your Prototype Has Served Its Purpose

  • You have shown it to at least 3-5 people and received consistent feedback
  • You know what the core value is — the one feature people actually care about
  • You have a list of what to build next based on real feedback, not assumptions
  • You have discovered deal-breakers — things that would need to work differently at scale
  • You are starting to hit limitations that conversational AI prototyping cannot solve easily

The Prototype IS the Product

Sometimes your prototype is good enough. This is more common than you might think, especially for:

  • Internal tools that only you or your team will use
  • Spreadsheet-based systems that handle your actual workflow
  • Simple websites or landing pages that do not need complex backend logic
  • Calculators and converters that solve a specific problem
  • Portfolio or presentation pieces that showcase your work

If your prototype works, people are using it, and it does not need to scale to thousands of users, there is no rule that says you have to rebuild it "properly." Use what works.

When to Bring In a Developer

Consider hiring a developer when:

  • Your idea needs user accounts, payments, or sensitive data handling
  • You need the tool to handle hundreds or thousands of simultaneous users
  • The prototype works but is too slow or unreliable for daily use
  • You need integrations with other services (payment processors, email systems, databases)
  • You are ready to turn this into a real business and need a solid foundation

The good news: your prototype is now the best possible brief for a developer. Instead of explaining an abstract idea, you can show them a working example and say "build this, but make it production-ready." That saves everyone time and money.

Pro Tip: When you do hire a developer, share the AI conversation history from your prototyping session. It contains all the decisions you made, the features you tried and discarded, and the specific behaviors you want. It is the most detailed project spec you will ever write, and you created it just by having a conversation.

Showing Your Prototype to Others

A prototype only becomes valuable when other people interact with it. Showing your work early is uncomfortable, but it is the entire point of building fast. Here is how to get the most out of sharing your prototype.

Frame It Correctly

Before you show anyone, set the right expectations. Say something like:

  • "This is a rough prototype I built in a few hours. I am not looking for feedback on the design — I want to know if the core idea is useful to you."
  • "Some things are broken and it looks rough. I want to know: would you actually use something like this?"
  • "I built this to test an idea. What is your first reaction when you see it?"

Framing protects you from two traps: people being too polite ("Oh, it looks great!") and people focusing on the wrong things ("That button should be more rounded").

Ask the Right Questions

Generic feedback is useless. Guide people toward the feedback you actually need:

  • "What would you expect to happen when you click this?" — reveals whether your design is intuitive
  • "If this existed right now, would you use it this week?" — tests real demand, not hypothetical interest
  • "What is the first thing you would change?" — surfaces priorities you might have missed
  • "What is confusing about this?" — identifies problems you are too close to see
  • "Who else do you know who might need this?" — tests whether the idea has legs beyond your immediate circle

Watch, Do Not Explain

The most valuable feedback comes from watching someone use your prototype without guidance. Hand it to them and say "Try this out" without explaining what it does. Where they get confused, where they hesitate, where they smile — that is the real feedback. If you have to explain how something works, it means the design needs to be clearer.

Tangible Beats Abstract Every Time

There is a reason investors prefer demos over pitch decks. When someone can touch, click, and interact with your idea, the conversation changes completely. They stop asking "But how would that work?" and start saying "What if it also did this?" That shift — from skepticism to collaboration — is what a prototype makes possible.

Exercise: Think of an idea you have been sitting on — it could be an app, a tool, a small website, a spreadsheet system, anything. Set a timer for one hour. Open a conversation with AI and describe your idea as clearly as you can. Follow the prototyping process from this lesson: get a first version, iterate in conversation, and build it in layers. When your hour is up, stop. Take what you have — rough, imperfect, maybe a little broken — and show it to one person. Ask them: "Would you use this?" Write down what they said, what surprised you, and what you would change for version two. That is the complete prototyping cycle, from idea to feedback, in a single afternoon.
